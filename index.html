I. About

Why I Build

My work is defined not by titles or years of formal experience, but by what I actually build — and more importantly, by why. Every project documented here began as a genuine personal need, and was turned into something real, functional, and built to last.

Chatme was not born from a desire to learn Flutter. It was born from a single, concrete necessity: a private chat application entirely free from third-party servers, subscriptions, and external dependencies — one I could own and control completely. That need drew me toward the Nostr decentralised protocol, its event-based relay architecture, and the NIP-04 encrypted messaging standard built upon the secp256k1 elliptic curve.

Research Note

Curiosity did not stop at usage. I began asking deeper questions: how does this curve actually function mathematically? What determines its specific parameters? What guarantees its security properties?

I studied the mathematical foundations of elliptic curve cryptography — not from a textbook, but through direct implementation and experimentation. I built primitives, deliberately broke them, studied the results, and rebuilt with greater precision. The work of Daniel J. Bernstein, particularly Curve25519, became a central reference — not just for its technical elegance, but for its commitment to transparency and verifiability. This iterative process gradually crystallised into a more ambitious question: what would it require to design an entirely original curve from first principles?

That question gave rise to Curve256189 — a fully original elliptic curve cryptography library built in Dart, achieving full SafeCurves compliance across all 11 criteria. The journey did not end there. Each lesson learned from Curve256189 directly shaped the design of its successor: Curve33617, a new Montgomery curve at the ~168-bit security level, built with even greater transparency from the very first commit. The long-term objective is for this work to serve as the cryptographic backbone of Chatme itself, fully independent of the Nostr ecosystem.

My methodology is simple: understand by building. I am not a senior developer, nor a professional mathematician. I am a builder — one who is deeply committed to the craft, to intellectual honesty, and to doing things properly.

Curve256189

An original elliptic curve cryptography library constructed from first principles in Dart, defined over the prime field p = 2^256 - 189. Achieves full SafeCurves compliance across all 11 criteria. The library is intended to serve as the independent cryptographic foundation of Chatme.

Research Note

The starting point was secp256k1 — the curve underlying Nostr, encountered during the construction of Chatme. That initial encounter sparked a deeper question: how does an elliptic curve actually work at the parameter level? Studying the work of Daniel J. Bernstein, particularly Curve25519, eventually led to a more ambitious one: what would it take to design an original curve from scratch? That question gave rise to Curve256189, and continues to drive the development of its successor, Curve33617.

Curve33617

A new Montgomery curve defined over the prime field p = 2^336 - 17, occupying a deliberate gap in the landscape of standardised elliptic curves at the ~168-bit security level — sitting precisely between Curve25519 (~128-bit) and Curve448 (~224-bit). All parameters are selected transparently and reproducibly via SageMath.

Research Note

Curve33617 was born from the lessons of Curve256189 — not as a correction, but as a fresh start with greater clarity. Every parameter decision is documented and verifiable from the first commit. The prime p = 2^336 - 17 was selected through systematic scan; the coefficient A is derived transparently via brute force with Twisted Edwards compatibility enforced from the outset.
